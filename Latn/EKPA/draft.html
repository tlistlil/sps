Standard Russian

The phonology of Russian is characterized by two topics: reduction of unstressed vowels and various forms of consonant palatalization.

Vowel reduction

The vowel domain ($Lbd) uses ±CPO to distinguish five phonemes (Table 1). The features ±ERT vary within each phoneme, creating allophones that convey suprasegmental information. ±WGN are all minus and contribute neither to phonemic nor to phonetic contrast. Some argue that there are six vowels in the language, according to which /i/ here would be split into /ü/ with +E and /i/ with −E.

Allophones of each vowel phoneme are divided into four by two dimensions (Table 3): whether it has lexical stress on it or not, and whether it is in a ‘hard’ position or in a ‘soft’ position. This division gives us enough approximation, though real allophones are continuous rather than binary (influenced by how far it is from stressed positions, whether it is in a morphological ending, ‘hardness’ or ‘softness’ of its preceding consonant, that of its following consonant, etc.).

Realization of EKPA features in allophones is given in Table 2. Stressed vowels are pronounced strong and long (+T), while unstressed ones weak and short (−T). Tone is used to express modality independent from lexical stress and is not discussed here. Lack of stress reduces vowel quality as well, contrast between phonemes being lost — only three sounds are distinguished in unstressed ‘hard’ positions, and only two in unstressed ‘soft’. Comparison between stressed and unstressed allophones in each phoneme indicates that a phoneme is inclined to gain +C and lose +POERT when it is reduced. This is predictable, as the features +POERT are all associated with greater articulatory effort to produce distinct sounds, whereas +C corresponds to a smaller, more relaxed mouth opening. Note that the acute mark on stressed vowels indicates the feature +R, not stress itself. This follows the EKPA principles on diacritics, although raisedness is indeed correlated with stressed allophones.

Most consonants in the language come in pairs of palatalized (+R, called ‘soft’ consonants) and unpalatalized (−R, called ‘hard’ consonants) ones (Table 4). While other languages, such as Irish with its broad/slender consonants and Japanese with yōon, exhibit similar phonemic distinctions, the hard/soft pairing in Russian is more thorough and systematic. All consonants without pair, apart from /y/, are historically palatalized dental or velar obstruents (/z̈ s̈ s̈́s̈́ ts ts̈́/).

A key phonotactic constraint in Russian is that only ‘soft’ consonants can precede front vowels or /y/, and only ‘hard’ consonants can precede back vowels. Different strategies fit phonemes into this rule: palatalizing or depalatalizing the consonant by switching the pairs, shifting the consonant to the ones outside of the pair, shifting the vowel to its allophones, or mixtures of them. One such process, iotation, is particularly noteworthy as it plays a crucial role in Russian morphology, including verb conjugation and word derivation.

Iotation occurs when a ‘hard’ consonant (or a cluster /sk st/) is followed by historical /y/ (*j, ‘iota’) (Table 5). The /y/ was typically deleted after changing the former into an unpaired consonant (which was historically ‘soft’, although not necessarily so in the modern Russian), but in the case of labials, the /y/ itself $LbDCpoeRwgnt turned to /ĺ/ $LBdcpoeRwgnt. ‘Hard’ consonants not listed in Table 5 are turned into their +R counterparts in pairs if followed by /y/. While iotation is a form of palatalization in a broad sense, it should be distinguished from the ‘first, second, and third palatalization’, which are specific historical sound changes in Slavic linguistics (that of velar consonants under other conditions).

A comparison of EKPA features before and after iotation reveals several key patterns (Table 6). At first glance, the outputs appear phonetically disparate: sibilants like /z̈/ for velars/alveolars, and the lateral /ĺ/ for labials. However, they are unified by a shared feature combination of $Bp. This suggests that these ‘bright’ and ‘medial’ phonemes emerged from transitional sounds between the input consonants and the following /y/.

Furthermore, the output values for features ±LCO are determined entirely by the input consonant’s primary place of articulation. Labial inputs ($Po) consistently yield an $Lco output (/ĺ/), while velar and alveolar inputs ($PO, $po) yield an $lCO output (sibilants). This choice is independent of other input features like manner (±BC) or voicing (±W). It corresponds to the selection of outputs such as /by/ → /bĺ/ (+L) over a hypothetical *bz̈ (−L), and /dy/ → /z̈/ (−L) over *dĺ (+L). The motivation for this pattern likely relates to articulatory economy, favoring the most efficient phonetic transition.

Finally, the feature ±W (voicing) is preserved for sibilant outputs but is neutralized to -W (voiced) for the lateral output. Consequently, iotation expanded the sibilant inventory with voiced/voiceless pairs (e.g., /z̈/, /s̈/) but only introduced a single voiced lateral /ĺ/, producing no voiceless counterpart like *l̊́.
