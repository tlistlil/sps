Standard Russian

The phonology of Russian is characterized by two topics: reduction of unstressed vowels and various forms of consonant palatalization.

Vowel reduction

The vowel domain ($Lbd) uses ±CPO to distinguish five phonemes (Table 1). The features ±ER vary within each phoneme, creating allophones that convey suprasegmental information. ±WGNT are all minus and contribute neither to phonemic nor to phonetic contrast. Some argue that there are six vowels in the language, according to which /I/ here would be split into /Ị/ with +E and /I/ with −E.

Allophones of each vowel phoneme are divided into four by two dimensions (Table 3): whether it has lexical stress on it or not, and whether it is in a ‘hard’ position or in a ‘soft’ position. This division gives us enough approximation, though real allophones are continuous rather than binary (influenced by how far it is from stressed positions, whether it is in a morphological ending, ‘hardness’ or ‘softness’ of its preceding consonant, that of its following consonant, etc.).

Realization of EKPA features in allophones is given in Table 2. Stressed vowels are pronounced strong and long, while unstressed ones weak and short. Tone is used to express modality independent from lexical stress and is not discussed here. Lack of stress reduces vowel quality as well, contrast between phonemes being lost — only three sounds are distinguished in unstressed ‘hard’ positions, and only two in unstressed ‘soft’. Comparison between stressed and unstressed allophones in each phoneme indicates that a phoneme is inclined to gain +C and lose +POER when it is reduced. This is predictable, as the features +POER are all associated with greater articulatory effort to produce distinct sounds, whereas +C corresponds to a smaller, more relaxed mouth opening.

Most consonants in the language come in pairs of palatalized (+R, called ‘soft’ consonants) and unpalatalized (−R, called ‘hard’ consonants) ones (Table 4). It partially resembles Irish with its broad/slender consonants, or Japanese with yōon. All consonants without pair, apart from /Y/, are historically palatalized dental or velar obstruents (/Ẓ Ṣ Ṣ̄́ C Ć̣/).

A key phonotactic constraint in Russian is that only ‘soft’ consonants can precede front vowels or /Y/, and only ‘hard’ consonants can precede back vowels. Different strategies fit phonemes into this rule: palatalizing or depalatalizing the consonant by switching the pairs, mutating the consonant to ones out of the pair, shifting the vowel to its allophones, or mixtures of them. One such process, iotation, is particularly noteworthy as it plays a crucial role in Russian morphology, including verb conjugation and word derivation.

Iotation occurs when a ‘hard’ consonant (or a cluster /SK ST/) precedes an abstract phoneme /¥/ (‘iota’) (Table 5). This process can be understood from both a diachronic and a synchronic perspective. Diachronically, it originates from a sound change around the fifth century where consonants assimilated to a following Proto-Balto-Slavic *j (≒ /Y/ $LbDCeRwgnt). This *j was subsequently deleted after altering the preceding phoneme. Synchronically, iotation functions as a regular morphophonological alteration in which certain morphemes are analyzed as containing an underlying /¥/. For most consonants, this /¥/ triggers the change and then disappears, but after a labial consonant, it surfaces as /Ĺ/ $LBdceRwgnt. While iotation is a form of palatalization in a broad sense, it should be distinguished from the ‘first, second, and third palatalization’, which are other specific historical sound changes in Slavic linguistics.

Comparison of EKPA features before and after iotation reveals several key patterns (Table 6). At first glance, the outputs appear phonetically disparate, with sibilants like /Ẓ/ and the lateral /Ĺ/ mixed together. However, they are unified by a shared feature combination of $Bp. This suggests that these ‘bright’ and ‘medial’ sounds emerge from transitional sounds between the input consonants and the following /¥/.

Furthermore, the output values for ±LCO are determined entirely by the input consonant’s place of articulation features. Labial inputs ($Po) consistently yield an $Lco output (/Ĺ/), while velar and alveolar inputs ($PO, $po) yield an $lCO output (sibilants), independent of other input features like manner (±BC) or voicing (±W). It corresponds to the selection of outputs such as /B¥/ → /BĹ/ (+L) over a hypothetical *BẒ (−L), and /D¥/ → /Ẓ/ (−L) over *DĹ (+L). The motivation for this pattern likely relates to articulatory economy.

Finally, ±W is preserved for sibilant outputs but is neutralized to -W (voiced) for the lateral output. Consequently, iotation expanded the sibilant inventory with both voiced and voiceless phonemes (e.g., /Ẓ Ṣ/) but only introduced a single voiced lateral /Ĺ/, producing no voiceless counterpart like *L̊́.
